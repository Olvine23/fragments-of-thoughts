I’m getting used to the smell of fresh berries sprouting in my yard. For a second, I mistook them for raspberries, but no, strawberries they were.

I watch her in that elegant red dress, picking them. She matches the fruit perfectly as they surround her, while I sit in the rocking chair my father left me, smiling.

I hold the armrest, trying to get up cautiously. I'm careful not to rip my stitches out, being so fresh from surgery. Last night, she was the surgeon stitching my soul, teaching my lips to kiss and my hands to hold—her. She made me realize I didn’t need everything; I only needed her.

God, she puzzles me. Is she a doctor, a farmer, or a teacher? Before I find out, I just want to thank Him for this gift. It was once a dream, but now it’s the reality that wakes me—teaching me to forget the poverty that had drained me down to a needle.

She has a perfect voice, and something else—she is my alarm. Whenever she whispers, "Dear, it’s dawn, get up," I confuse her for the morning birds.

She’s the only one who sings to me, beauty in my ears. When she does, I gasp in awe because she’s right there, sitting on my floor. I’m afraid someone might sting me—I think my eyes are still closed, dreaming. But no, please don't; for the door might abruptly open and she might stand and close it from the other side, gone.

I don't want that. I want to be with her, to love her and have her. She is a lot in one, and she is just for me.
